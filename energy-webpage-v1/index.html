Appliance Energy Consumption in Australia

Explore how everyday household appliances consume energy in Australian homes. Understanding energy usage helps reduce electricity bills and environmental impact.

Why energy consumption matters

Australian households spend an average of $1,700 per year on electricity. Appliances account for a significant portion of this cost. Understanding which appliances use the most energy helps consumers make smarter, more sustainable choices.

Average household

An average Australian home uses around 18–20 kWh of electricity per day across all appliances.

Biggest energy users

Heating and cooling, water heaters, and refrigerators are the top energy consumers in most homes.

Reduce your bill

Switching to energy-efficient appliances with high star ratings can cut energy costs by up to 30%.

About the energy star rating system

The Australian energy star rating system helps consumers compare the energy efficiency of appliances. More stars means more efficient. The system is mandatory for many appliances sold in Australia including televisions, refrigerators, and washing machines.


When shopping for a new TV, choosing a model with a higher star rating can save hundreds of dollars over the lifetime of the product while also reducing your household carbon footprint.


💡 Energy saving tip: Turning off your TV at the wall instead of leaving it on standby can save up to $9 per year per TV.

Finding 1: LCD (LED) dominates the Australian TV market

Research question

What screen technologies are currently available in Australia and which are the most frequent?

→
Data needed

→
Chart type

Bar chart — best for comparing counts across a small number of categories with clear labels.

→
Expected finding

LCD (LED) will dominate. Understanding market share informs how energy studies should weight technology types.

Context

Understanding the distribution of screen technologies is fundamental to any research into TV energy consumption. Technology type is a primary determinant of power consumption characteristics.


What the data shows

LCD (LED) technology accounts for 3,747 models — over 80% of all available TVs in Australia. OLED accounts for 293 models (6%) while traditional LCD accounts for 485 models (10%).


Research implication

Any research into Australian TV energy consumption must account for the overwhelming dominance of LCD (LED) technology. Stratified sampling is recommended when comparing technologies.

Brand concentration in consumer electronics markets has significant implications for energy efficiency research. When a small number of brands control the majority of models, their design choices disproportionately influence overall market energy consumption.


What the data shows

The Australian TV market shows significant brand concentration. The top brands including Hisense, Samsung, LG, and Sony collectively account for a substantial majority of available models.

Researchers and policy makers should prioritise engagement with the top 5–10 brands. Changes to manufacturing standards for these brands would affect the majority of models available to Australian consumers.

Finding 3: Screen size is a strong predictor of power consumption

Research question

What is the relationship between screen size and power consumption across available TV models?

→

Expected finding

Positive correlation between size and power. Variation within categories will reveal efficiency differences beyond just size.

Context

Understanding the physical determinants of TV power consumption is essential for developing accurate energy consumption models. Screen size represents the primary physical characteristic that researchers and consumers can observe and compare.


What the data shows

There is a clear positive relationship between screen size and power consumption. As screen diagonal increases from 22 to 85 inches, power consumption increases substantially. However significant variation within each size category indicates technology and manufacturing efficiency also play important roles.


Research implication

Screen size should be included as a covariate in any statistical model of TV energy consumption. Researchers should investigate which design factors explain the within-category variation.

Conclusions and research recommendations

📊

Market structure

The Australian TV market is dominated by LCD (LED) technology and a small number of major brands. Research designs should account for this concentration to avoid sampling bias.

🔋

Energy drivers

Screen size is a primary driver of power consumption but does not fully explain variation. Future research should investigate panel efficiency, backlighting technology, and smart TV features.

🎯

Policy targeting

Engaging with the top 5–10 brands and focusing on LCD (LED) efficiency improvements would have the greatest impact given their market dominance.

📋

Data limitations

The dataset may not include all available models and energy consumption may vary with real-world usage. Longitudinal studies would strengthen these findings.

About the data

Data source

Australian Government energy rating dataset (tv_2026_03_26.csv). Contains TV models registered for sale in Australia including brand, screen technology, screen size, power consumption, and energy star rating.

Data processing

Cleaned using KNIME — removing duplicates, filtering to available Australian models, standardising brand names to uppercase, and converting screen size from centimetres to inches.

Privacy

The dataset contains no personal or sensitive information. It focuses solely on product specifications and energy consumption data.

Accuracy and limitations

The dataset may not include all available TV models. Energy consumption may vary depending on real-world usage conditions such as brightness settings and content type.
